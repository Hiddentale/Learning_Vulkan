Problem is confirmed to be in the simulation (maybe resample.rs)

Subducting plates keep rotating. 
Every timestep, their oceanic points slide further across the sphere.
Physically, real crust would descend into the mantle and disappear. 
But here the point just stays on the surface. 
After enough steps, a subducting plate's "leading edge" has drifted past the 
trench and now sits within the overriding plate's territory as ghost points.

So at any active convergent boundary you end up with two layers of points 
from different plates occupying nearly the same region of the sphere.

At resample (resample.rs:71), each new Fibonacci point inherits 
the plate of the nearest old point. 
In the ghost-overlap zone, "nearest" is essentially noise.
Sometimes the overriding plate's point wins, 
sometimes the subducted ghost point wins.
Depending on which happens to be microscopically closer. 
Result: salt-and-pepper plate assignment exactly where you see fragmentation.

And it snowballs: once a Fibonacci point gets mis-tagged 
with the subducted plate's ID, that plate's rotation carries 
it on the subducted plate's trajectory, widening the smear each cycle.
